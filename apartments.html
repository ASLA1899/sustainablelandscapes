Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments

San Francisco, California, U.S.A.
Close Me!

In 2009, 62 percent of San Francisco’s homeless population was chronically homeless. While San Francisco’s homeless crisis is not worse than those in other large cities, it’s one of the most visible due to the temperate climate.

Photo: Brant Ward / The San Francisco Chronicle

Photo 1 of 12

Close Me!

For more than a decade, the San Francisco city government has undertaken a $1.5 billion plan to end chronic homelessness by more effectively connecting chronically-homeless people to permanent, supportive housing. Between 2004 and 2015 the city built nearly 2,700 units of long-term supportive housing and permanently housed 11,362 formerly-homeless people, making it a leader in its response to homelessness.

Photo: Richardson Apartments, view from Fulton Street / DBArchitects

Photo 2 of 12

Close Me!

On a half-acre lot in Mid-market San Francisco, Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments is a new home for chronically homeless adults. The 120-unit, GreenPoint-rated building, which is run by the Community Housing Partnership and financed by multiple public agencies, offers services such as a counseling center, medical center, job training program, and employment through the building’s bakery and cafés.

Photo: ASLA 2012 Professional Residential Design Award of Excellence. Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments / Bruce

Photo 3 of 12

Close Me!

The five-story building complex is found on a former parking lot near bus and subway lines. There are also many services within walking distance. Access to transportation is extremely important to its low-income residents who are without cars.

Photo: ASLA 2012 Professional Residential Design Award of Excellence. Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments / Bruce Damonte

Photo 4 of 12

Close Me!

A system was developed by Andrea Cochran Landscape Architecture to maximize the infiltration of stormwater in the apartment complex. This was critical for San Francisco’s combined sewer and stormwater system, which has a tendency to overflow. An innovative streetscape includes permeable pavers and rain gardens, which make the public space more attractive while reducing stormwater runoff on the site.

Photo: Andrea Cochran Landscape Architecture

Photo 5 of 12

Close Me!

A shady central courtyard created in the core of the building accommodates multiple uses within a single space. A large patio with custom tables, benches, and a barbeque is used for communal gatherings, while private areas provide seating for individuals or smaller groups.

Photo: ASLA 2012 Professional Residential Design Award of Excellence. Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments / Bruce Damonte

Photo 6 of 12

Close Me!

Giant chain ferns, Japanese painted ferns, western sword fern, and wood sorrel were used throughout the interior landscape to create the feeling of a peaceful grove. These plants are durable, low-maintenance, and adaptable to the extreme light conditions of full sun or deep shade.

Photo: ASLA 2012 Professional Residential Design Award of Excellence. Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments / Bruce Damonte

Photo 7 of 12

Close Me!

Within the courtyard, permeable spacers were installed between unit pavers to allow stormwater to infiltrate into a gravel retention basin constructed below. Other paving areas slope into rain gardens, which are planted with palm trees and ferns.

Photo: ASLA 2012 Professional Residential Design Award of Excellence. Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments / Bruce Damonte

Photo 8 of 12

Close Me!

A roof-top deck offers another healing space for residents, with seating areas, succulent gardens, raised beds for vegetable gardening, surrounded by sedum-filled green roof. Planters, both ornamental and edible, capture water that would otherwise enter the city’s storm system.

Photo: ASLA 2012 Professional Residential Design Award of Excellence. Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments / Bruce Damonte

Close Me!

About one-third of the building's residents are in wheelchairs, so incorporating full accessibility into the design was important. The courtyard is completely accessible, with cantilevered tables and spaces for wheelchairs adjacent to and across from benches.

Photo: ASLA 2012 Professional Residential Design Award of Excellence. Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments / Bruce Damonte

Close Me!

Monterey cypress logs used to construct the benches and block seats were reclaimed from urban forests throughout San Francisco. Locally-sourced black basalt gravel was used in drainage areas located under benches in the courtyard, at the perimeter of the building, and between the joints of the courtyard pavers. The unit pavers were also created in California.

Photo: ASLA 2012 Professional Residential Design Award of Excellence. Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments / Bruce Damonte

Project Facts

  • Drs. Julian and Raye Richardson Apartments first opened its doors to tenants in September 2011. The building includes 120 residential studio units for San Francisco’s chronically homeless, offering them a peaceful respite with a courtyard and rooftop garden.
  • The apartment complex saves San Francisco city government $2.4 million a year in costs associated with services for chronically homeless individuals as well as water conservation.
  • The complex’s landscape was designed to maximize rainwater infiltration on the sandy site.
  • The green streetscape includes permeable pavers and rain gardens.
  • Roughly one-third of the building's residents are in wheelchairs, so shared spaces are fully accessible.
